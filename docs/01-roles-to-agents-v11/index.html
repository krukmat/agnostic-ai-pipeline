# From roles to agents: orchestrating PO/BA/Architect/Dev/QA

Lightweight contracts and predictable handoffs

**TL;DR**

  • Define each role (PO/BA/Architect/Dev/QA) with a one-page YAML contract.
  • Keep agents small; let files and CI enforce consistency.
  • Use it as a PoC your team can adopt in a day and refine over time.

Clear handoffs beat clever prompts. Once each role has a tiny contract—inputs, outputs, and limits—the whole pipeline gets easier to reason about. This post shows how to write those contracts so developers can use them, extend them, and keep them honest.

Why now: the AI, economic, and cultural backdrop

  • **Models change weekly.** Capabilities, context windows, and tool APIs keep shifting. Designing **model‑agnostic** flows protects your team from churn.
  • **Budgets are tight.** You need fast **proof‑of‑value** and early **cost controls** (caps, routing, and latency targets).
  • **Distributed teams are the norm.** Clear artifacts and handoffs reduce onboarding time and unblock async work.
  • **Regulatory pressure is rising.** Keep data boundaries, simple audit trails, and reproducible outputs.

Working in a strengths/weaknesses model landscape

  • **Trade‑offs**: cost vs. quality vs. latency vs. context length vs. safety filters.
  • **IP & privacy**: understand usage terms; prefer local for drafts, escalate to cloud when justified.
  • **API volatility**: adapters and circuit breakers keep the pipeline stable while providers evolve.

What a software‑factory pipeline buys you

  • **Repeatability**: the same inputs produce inspectable artifacts (YAML, markdown).
  • **Modularity**: roles as contracts; swap an agent or model without a rewrite.
  • **Measurement**: budget/latency tracked per step; outliers are obvious.
  • **Governance**: allowlists, validators, and bounded actions by default.
  • **Community**: a shared, open PoC others can fork, improve, and reuse.

The problem

Without clear contracts, people guess. Context gets duplicated across prompts, decisions are implicit, and quality checks drift. The result is rework: someone interprets a paragraph differently and the chain breaks.

A simple way forward

Move the ambiguity into tiny, explicit contracts. For each role:

  • **Inputs/Outputs.** What you need and what you produce (files, fields, formats).
  • **Limits.** Scope and guardrails (e.g., max tokens, allowed actions, budget caps).
  • **Acceptance.** A short checklist that CI (or a script) can verify.

Start with one or two roles; ship something small; evolve from there.

Example


# templates/roles/ba.yaml
role: "BA"
inputs:
  - file: "planning/requirements.yaml"
outputs:
  - file: "planning/normalized_requirements.yaml"
limits:
  max_tokens: 2000
  forbidden_actions:
    - "write outside planning/"
acceptance:
  - "normalized_requirements.yaml exists"
  - "all sections populated (title, scope, constraints)"

Key takeaways

  • Contracts cut ambiguity and make agents replaceable (by a person or a different model).
  • Small scope → faster iteration and fewer surprises.
  • Acceptance checks make the pipeline self-policing.
  • You can adopt one role at a time—no big bang.

What to do next

  • Copy the role templates and write your first BA → Architect pass on a real requirement.
  • Commit the contracts and wire a tiny CI check (existence + basic schema).
  • Open an issue with your edge cases; propose improvements to the templates.
  • Share a short before/after; other teams can fork and refine it with you.
